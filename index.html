Why it matters

Results sites tell you who won. Not what they played.

Comp data has always been the missing piece — scattered across five regional broadcasts, gone the moment the stream ends. This is where it lives instead.

Map-specific truth

A hero that wins on King's Row can flop on Busan. Every rate is map-scoped, and the maps-played count is always in view.

Only what's proven

A comp appears only when it traces to a frame, a crop and a match score. Anything uncertain says needs review — never a silent guess.

Under the hood

Fed by an evidence pipeline.

A computer-vision pipeline calibrates each broadcast, reads the ten hero slots frame by frame, and stages every detection through human review before it counts. That's the back office — you don't need it to use the tracker, but it's all here if you want the receipts.

FAQ

Questions, answered.

Where does the comp data come from?

From the OWCS broadcasts themselves. After a match finishes, the pipeline reviews the VOD, samples frames of live gameplay, and identifies the hero icons shown for both teams. Match results and schedules come from public OWCS/FACEIT pages.

How is “win rate” defined here?

A hero counts as played on a map if it appeared in any recorded snapshot for that team on that map. Win rate is the share of those maps the team won. Comps change mid-map, so treat low sample sizes with care — the maps-played column is always shown.

What happens when the pipeline isn't sure?

It says so. Every detection carries a per-slot confidence score. Low scores are labeled needs review and stay out of the stats until a human confirms or corrects them.
